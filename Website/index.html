Overview about Earth's Moon

The brightest and largest object in our night sky, the Moon makes Earth a more livable planet.
Earth's Moon is the fifth largest of the 200+ moons orbiting planets in our solar system.
The average distance from Earth to the Moon is about 238,855 miles (384,400 km).
Formation: The prevailing theory is that the Moon formed about 4.5 billion years ago from debris produced by a giant impact between the early Earth and a Mars-sized body named Theia.

Physical characteristics of the moon

Size

With a radius of about 1,080 miles (1,740 kilometers), the Moon is less than a third of the width of Earth.

Surface Composition

it is made of Basaltic Rocks, Anorthosites, Breccias, Regolith, Water Ice, oxygen, silicon, magnesium, iron, calcium, and aluminum, Helium-3.

Atmosphere

The Moon has a very thin and weak atmosphere, called an exosphere. It does not provide any protection from the Sun's radiation or impacts from meteoroids.

Temperature

The temperature on the moon can reach a blistering 250° Fahrenheit (120° Celsius or 400 Kelvin) during lunar daytime at the moon's equator, and plummet to -208 degrees F (-130° C, 140 K) at night.

Gravity

1/6th as strong as Earth's. If you weigh 60 kg on Earth, you'd weigh just 10 kg on the Moon.

Water Ice at the Poles

There are regions at the Moon's poles where sunlight never reaches. Recent missions have discovered evidence of water ice in these permanently shadowed regions.

Human landing (Apollo missions)

Apollo 11 (July 20, 1969)

This is the most famous Moon landing. Neil Armstrong and Edwin "Buzz" Aldrin became the first and second humans to walk on the Moon.

Apollo 12 (November 19-20, 1969)

This mission landed closer to its intended target than Apollo 11.

Apollo 14 (February 5-6, 1971)

This was Alan Shepard's second spaceflight; he was the first American in space in 1961.

Apollo 15 (July 30 - August 2, 1971)

This was the first of the "J missions" which had stayed on the Moon longer. David R. Scott and James B. Irwin spent three days on the lunar surface.

Apollo 16 (April 21-23, 1972)

John W. Young and Charles M. Duke explored the Moon for three days. Thomas K. Mattingly remained in lunar orbit. This mission focused on geological explorations.

Apollo 17 (December 11-14, 1972)

Eugene A. Cernan and Harrison H. Schmitt spent three days on the Moon, Eugene Cernan is the last human to have walked on the Moon as of 2021.

Formation theories

Ancient Theories

  • Cosmic Egg Theory: In some ancient cultures, like in China, it was believed that the Moon came into being from a cosmic egg. After the egg cracked open, the heavier parts formed the Earth and the lighter parts rose to become the Moon.
  • Capture Theory: ancient thinkers believed that the Moon was a wandering celestial body that the Earth captured
  • Mother Earth Theory: Some ancient myths, such as those from Greece, saw the Moon as a sibling to the Earth, birthed from primordial entities like Gaia or Uranus.

Modern Theories

  • Fission Theory: in the 19th century, it is suggested that the Moon was once part of the Earth and was separated from it.
  • Capture Theory (modern version): This theory proposes that the Moon was formed somewhere else in the solar system and was captured by Earth's gravity.
  • Double Planet Hypothesis: This suggests that both Earth and the Moon formed together as a double system from the primordial solar nebula. However, the significant differences in composition between the Earth and the Moon challenge this theory.
  • Giant Impact Hypothesis (or Theia Impact): This is the most widely accepted theory today. It proposes that a Mars-sized body, often named Theia, collided with the early Earth about 4.5 billion years ago. The debris from this impact eventually coalesced to form the Moon.

Apollo Packages (ALSEPs)

Were a unique series of geophysical experiments, which included seismic experiments. The experiments included

  • Seismic Experiment (PSE): were performed at Apollo 11, 12, 14, 15, and 16, to detect natural seismic events.
  • Active Seismic Experiment (ASE): were performed at stations 14 and 16, to evaluate the subsurface structure around the landing site using controlled seismic sources.
  • Lunar Seismic Profiling Experiment (LSPE): was performed at Apollo 17, to explore the subsurface down to a few kilometers, which was much deeper than the previous active seismic experiments.
  • Lunar Surface Gravimeter (LSG): was originally designed to detect gravitational waves on the Moon, and taking advantage of the very low noise conditions.

The moon is seismically active !

A "moonquake" is a seismic event or vibration on the Moon, it is the result of energy being released in the Moon's interior, causing the ground to shake.

Kinds of moonquakes

(1) deep moonquakes about 700 km below the surface, probably caused by tides.
(2) vibrations from the impact of meteorites.
(3) thermal quakes caused by the expansion of the frigid crust when first illuminated by the morning sun after two weeks of deep-freeze lunar night.
(4) shallow moonquakes only 20 or 30 kilometers below the surface.

  • The first three were generally mild and harmless.
  • Shallow moonquakes on the other hand were doozies.
  • Between 1972 and 1977, the Apollo seismic network saw twenty-eight of them.
  • a few registered up to 5.5 on the Richter scale.

Causes of Moonquakes ?

  • Earth’s gravity: the pull of Earth’s gravity tugging and stretching the Moon’s internal structures.
  • shrinkage : The Moon is shrinking as its interior cools, causing disturbance in the moon results in quakes.
  • Meteoroid impacts: Because the Moon has no atmosphere, meteoroids colliding with the Moon reach the surface without ‘burning out’ Only a very small fraction of a meteoroid's kinetic energy is converted to seismic energy.
  • Thermal expansion and contraction of rocks: as it goes from the frigid lunar night to the very hot lunar daytime, so that results in moonquakes.

Moonquakes VS Earthquakes

Certainly! Both "moonquakes" and "earthquakes" refer to seismic activities, but as their names suggest, they occur on different celestial bodies. The main two differences are:

Moonquakes last longer than Earthquakes !

Earthquakes last for from 10 to 30 seconds and the biggest one lasts for 2 minutes , but all moonquakes last for 10 minutes.

Why ?

The reason is that "Water weakens stone" When energy propagates across such a compressible structure, it acts like a foam sponge, so it deadens the vibrations.

Moonquakes are weaker than Earthquakes !

The strongest moonquake, which is 5.5 Richter , 5 Richter on Earth can only move heavy furniture and crack plaster.

Why ?

Quakes occur because of energy released when the tectonic plates slide over each other or break ,and because the tectonic plates of Earth are more complex than those of moon , so Moonquakes are weaker than Earthquakes.

Future direction in research due to Moonquakes

In the coming years, NASA plans to land more seismometers on more parts of the moon to learn about the frequency and severity of quakes. Without more information, lunar colonists could be in more danger and stress if they settle in a moonquake-prone region!

Moon internal structure

Crust :

The crust is the outermost layer of the moon, and it consists of a dusty outer rock layer which is known as the regolith. The regolith is a rocky layer that looks like broken concrete which has been blasted apart and fused back together. The crust is approximately 37 miles in thickness on the side which faces the earth and approximately 62 miles on the other side. The rocks found on the crust are almost the same as those found on the crust of the earth. The only difference is the fact that the rocks have more aluminum and titanium than those found on earth. The moon's crust also contains some magnetism like the crust of the earth. Studies show that this layer mainly comprises of silicon, iron, magnesium, oxygen, calcium, and aluminum.

Mantle:

This is the layer between the core and the crust of the moon. The mantle is divided into two parts. The first layer of the mantle is the rigid lithosphere (upper mantle). This layer of the moon is estimated to be 620 miles in thickness, and it is not hot enough to flow. The second layer of the mantle is the asthenosphere (lower mantle). The asthenosphere is not rigid like the upper mantle. The innermost parts of the asthenosphere are hot enough to flow. The asthenosphere layer can transmit seismic waves which cause quakes. The mantle is believed to be largely comprised of olivine, orthopyroxene and clinopyroxene minerals.

Core:

• The Moon's core is much smaller relative to its volume than Earth's core. It's not entirely well-defined in terms of its size and composition.
• The core is believed to be partially molten and consists primarily of iron, with smaller amounts of sulfur and nickel.
It can be further divided into:
• Inner Core: Solid and comprises mainly iron. It's estimated to have a radius of about 240 km, though estimates can vary.
• Outer Core: Surrounds the inner core and is thought to be molten, with a radius extending roughly to about 500 km from the Moon's center.
• The moon's core makes up only 2-4% of the total mass of the moon. This layer does not create a big magnetic field. The magnetic field of the moon is estimated to be 1/10 millionth of the magnetic field of the earth.

Why did we discover the moon structure?

Understanding the structure of the Moon isn't just about satisfying human curiosity, but there are many reasons to discover that:

  • Origin of the Moon: One of the primary motivations for studying the Moon's structure is to gain insights into its origin. The leading theory about the Moon's formation is the Giant Impact Hypothesis, which suggests that a Mars-sized body, often referred to as Theia, collided with the early Earth. Debris from this collision eventually coalesced to form the Moon. Understanding the Moon's interior can provide evidence for or against this theory.
  • Seismic Activity: Discovering and understanding "moonquakes" (seismic activity on the Moon) is crucial for any plans of long-term human habitation. If humans plan to establish a base or colony on the Moon, it's important to know where and how often these quakes occur.
  • Earth-Moon Comparative Studies: The Earth and the Moon are closely tied celestial bodies. By understanding the Moon's structure and composition, scientists can also draw conclusions about Earth's early history and its geological processes.
  • Preparation for Future Missions: As we look to the future, understanding the Moon's structure and geology is critical for planning new missions, whether robotic or crewed.

How did they discover the moon layers ?

The discovery and understanding of the Moon's internal layers have come through a combination of methods:

  • • Seismic Data from Apollo Missions: structure are known from seismometers placed on its surface during apollo missions. These seismometers detected moonquakes and the vibrations caused by meteoroid impacts. By analyzing how seismic waves travel through the Moon (their speeds, paths, and the ways they reflect off or get absorbed by internal layers), scientists can infer details about the Moon's interior.
  • • Study of Lunar Samples: Rock and soil samples brought back from the Apollo missions have given scientists direct evidence of the Moon's crust composition.
  • • Remote Sensing and Electromagnetic Studies: Instruments aboard lunar orbiters can provide insights into the Moon's composition, temperature, and other properties by studying how it emits or reflects different wavelengths of light, radio waves, or other electromagnetic radiation.
  • • Gravity Measurements: Orbital missions around the Moon, like the Gravity Recovery and Interior Laboratory (GRAIL) mission, have provided detailed gravity field maps of the Moon. Differences in gravitational strength can be used to infer density variations within the Moon, giving clues about its internal structure.
